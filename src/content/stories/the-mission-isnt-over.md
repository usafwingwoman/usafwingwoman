There's a moment every veteran knows. The one where the uniform comes off for the last time and the world outside the gate looks nothing like what you trained for. No mission brief. No chain of command. No clarity about what *excellent* even means anymore.

I stood in that moment at 23 years old, holding a discharge certificate and a set of skills that the civilian world had no language for. Mission Crew Commander. Electronic Attack. Top Secret clearance. Chief of Security. None of it translated cleanly into a résumé bullet. But every single bit of it was *real* — forged under conditions most people will never encounter.

What I've learned in the years since is that the mission never actually ended. It changed shape. The crew got smaller. The aircraft grounded. But the discipline, the decisiveness, the ability to hold composure when everything is moving fast and the stakes are high — that came with me. It always does. That's what service builds, if you let it. Not a chapter. A foundation.
